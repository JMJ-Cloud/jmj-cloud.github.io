*For enterprise IT leaders rethinking the build vs. buy equation in 2026*

---

Nine years ago, I founded JMJ Cloud with [Matt Paine](https://www.linkedin.com/in/matt-paine-a12ab17/) and [Jon Dixon](https://www.linkedin.com/in/dixonjd/) based on a straightforward thesis: enterprises should get more out of the ERP they already own, not keep buying more software. Between the three of us, we bring over 60 years of Oracle experience, including Jon's 18 years at Oracle Consulting and Matt's two decades implementing Oracle ERP across global organizations. I had seen firsthand how companies were drowning in SaaS subscriptions and bolt-on products that never quite fit their business. I believed there was a better way: build targeted extensions on top of the platform you already have.

The pushback was immediate and predictable. "Building custom software takes too long and costs too much. Why wouldn't I just buy something off the shelf?" And for a long time, that was a fair objection. Custom development required large teams, long timelines, and specialized expertise that most organizations struggled to maintain. The economics genuinely favored buying.

That objection no longer holds. Artificial intelligence has fundamentally altered every variable in the build vs. buy formula. The cost of building has plummeted. The speed of development has accelerated dramatically. And the hidden costs of buying, from escalating license fees to vendor lock-in, have never been more visible. The thesis I founded JMJ Cloud on nine years ago is no longer contrarian. It is becoming the consensus.

It is time for CTOs and CIOs to revisit their assumptions.

## The Traditional Case for COTS (And Why It Made Sense)

The dominance of COTS software was never irrational. Enterprise organizations faced genuine constraints that made buying the pragmatic choice:

**Time to market.** Building a custom ERP, CRM, or supply chain system from scratch could take years. Buying one meant going live in months.

**Expertise gap.** Not every company could attract and retain the caliber of software engineers needed to build mission-critical systems. Vendors employed thousands of specialists.

**Ongoing support.** COTS came with dedicated support teams, regular updates, and a community of users who had already encountered and solved most problems.

**Risk mitigation.** Custom software projects had notoriously high failure rates. A Standish Group report famously found that only 29% of IT projects were considered successful. Buying reduced perceived risk.

These factors created a self-reinforcing cycle. The more companies bought, the more COTS vendors invested in their products, making them harder to compete with internally. For a generation of IT leaders, "nobody ever got fired for buying SAP" became an unwritten rule.

## How AI Rewrites the Equation

The emergence of AI-assisted software development has disrupted every pillar of the traditional COTS argument.

**Development speed has transformed.** A peer-reviewed study published on arXiv found that developers using GitHub Copilot completed coding tasks 55.8% faster than those without it. GitHub's own research confirms that 84% of developers now use or plan to use AI tools in their workflow, and approximately 41% of all code written in 2025 was AI-generated. These are not marginal improvements. They represent a structural shift in how fast software can be built.

**The expertise barrier has fallen.** Tools like GitHub Copilot, Anthropic's Claude Code, and Cursor act as force multipliers, enabling smaller teams to produce output that previously required much larger ones. A mid-sized enterprise no longer needs a 50-person development team to build a custom application. A team of 10, augmented by AI, can achieve comparable results.

**Low-code and no-code platforms amplify the effect.** Gartner projected that by 2025, 70% of new enterprise applications would use low-code or no-code technologies, up from less than 25% in 2020. Platforms like Oracle APEX (included free with Oracle Database licenses) and Microsoft Power Apps (now with 56 million monthly active users, per Forrester's 2025 Wave report) allow business analysts and citizen developers to build functional applications without writing traditional code. Combined with AI assistance, these platforms make custom development accessible to a far broader talent pool.

The net effect: the primary advantage COTS held over custom development, faster delivery with less risk, has eroded dramatically.

## The Compounding Cost of COTS

While building has gotten cheaper, buying has gotten more expensive. And the trajectory is alarming.

In 2025, every major SaaS vendor raised prices significantly. Salesforce implemented a 6% increase on Enterprise and Unlimited editions, its second major hike in two years following a 9% portfolio-wide increase in 2023. Microsoft raised prices across multiple product lines simultaneously, including a 40% increase on Power BI Pro (from $10 to $14 per user per month) and 10–15% increases on Dynamics 365 Business Central. Google Workspace prices jumped 17–22% across all plans. Atlassian raised Data Center pricing by 15–30% depending on user tier.

This is not a one-time adjustment. According to data from Vertice, SaaS inflation is running at four times the rate of general inflation, with businesses now spending an average of $8,700 per employee on SaaS annually, a 27% year-over-year increase. Enterprise software support costs alone typically run 15–20% of license cost each year, and that percentage grows as user bases expand.

Over a 10-year horizon, the total cost of ownership for COTS solutions compounds relentlessly. Per-user pricing means every new hire, contractor, or acquired company increases your software bill. Custom software, by contrast, has a fixed development cost and marginal ongoing maintenance expenses. The cost curve inverts dramatically over time.

## The Customization Tax

Perhaps the most insidious hidden cost of COTS is what might be called the "customization tax." Every enterprise has unique processes, workflows, and requirements. COTS vendors design for the broadest possible market, which means their products inevitably require customization to fit any specific organization.

That customization comes at a premium. COTS vendors and their implementation partners charge significant fees for configuration, custom modules, and integrations. Worse, those customizations often break during upgrades, creating a recurring cost that never goes away. Organizations find themselves trapped in a cycle: they need the customization to make the software useful, but the customization makes them dependent on the vendor (or expensive consultants) to maintain it.

When you build in-house, those "customizations" are simply features. They are designed into the architecture from the start, maintained by your team, and evolved on your schedule. There is no upgrade cycle that threatens to break your workflows. There is no vendor deciding to deprecate the API your critical integration depends on.

## Data Ownership and Privacy at Stake

SaaS-based COTS means your data lives on someone else's infrastructure. In an era of increasing regulatory scrutiny (GDPR, CCPA, and sector-specific requirements in healthcare and financial services) this creates real risk.

When your customer data, financial records, and intellectual property reside in a vendor's cloud, you are dependent on their security posture, their compliance practices, and their data handling policies. A 2025 Onapsis report found a 210% increase in active exploitation of SAP vulnerabilities from 2024 to 2025. The attack surface of major enterprise platforms is large, well-documented, and actively targeted.

Custom software built on your own infrastructure (or your own cloud tenancy) gives you direct control over data residency, encryption, access policies, and audit trails. For industries where data sovereignty is not optional, this alone can tip the build vs. buy decision.

## Vendor Lock-in: The Strategic Risk Nobody Prices In

Forrester Research's 2025 analysis of major enterprise software vendors put it bluntly: vendors are using AI to "rebundle" their products and deepen lock-in. The report warned that consolidating to a primary platform "concentrates risk and neuters your negotiation leverage."

The risks are concrete:

**Price increases you cannot avoid.** Once your business processes, data, and integrations are deeply embedded in a vendor's ecosystem, switching costs become prohibitive. Vendors know this and price accordingly.

**Feature deprecation.** Vendors routinely sunset features, APIs, and product lines based on their own strategic priorities, not yours. When Atlassian aggressively pushed customers from Server to Cloud (and eventually Data Center), organizations had no choice but to comply or undertake expensive migrations.

**Acquisition risk.** When your COTS vendor gets acquired by a competitor or a private equity firm, your roadmap is no longer in the hands of the team that built the product. Priorities shift. Support degrades. Prices rise.

Custom software eliminates vendor lock-in entirely. You own the code, the architecture, and the roadmap. Your technology strategy is driven by your business needs, not a vendor's quarterly earnings targets.

## AI-Assisted Development: The New Reality

The tools available to enterprise development teams in 2026 would have been science fiction five years ago.

**GitHub Copilot** is now used by the majority of professional developers, with studies showing 20–40% individual productivity gains and significant time savings on routine coding and testing tasks.

**Claude Code and similar AI coding agents** can scaffold entire applications, write tests, generate documentation, and debug complex issues. They enable developers to work at a higher level of abstraction, focusing on architecture and business logic rather than boilerplate.

**Cursor and other AI-native IDEs** provide real-time, context-aware code generation that understands your entire codebase. New team members become productive faster, and existing team members move between projects with less ramp-up time.

**Bain & Company's 2025 technology report** found that leading organizations are not just using AI for coding speed. They are redirecting the time saved into higher-value work, ensuring that efficiency gains become business gains. The firms seeing the greatest returns are those who treat AI not as a tool for doing the same work faster, but as an enabler of work that was previously impractical.

The practical implication: a custom application that might have taken 18 months and $2 million to build in 2020 can now be delivered in 6–9 months at a fraction of the cost. That changes the math fundamentally.

## Custom Software as Competitive Advantage

Here is the argument that COTS can never answer: if every company in your industry uses the same software, configured in roughly the same way, where is the competitive advantage?

McKinsey's research on digital transformation is clear: the real competitive advantage comes from building organizational and technological capabilities to broadly innovate, deploy, and improve solutions at scale. Software that is uniquely tailored to your processes, your customers, and your strategy is a moat. Software that your competitors can buy with a purchase order is not.

Consider the companies that have built lasting advantages through custom technology:

**Amazon** did not buy a warehouse management system off the shelf. They built one that became so efficient it turned into a business. Amazon Web Services grew from internal infrastructure into a $100 billion revenue engine.

**Netflix** built its own recommendation engine, content delivery network (Open Connect), and custom UI frameworks. These are not capabilities you can buy from a vendor. They are the reason Netflix dominates streaming.

**Capital One** made a deliberate decision years ago to become a technology company that happens to do banking. By building proprietary software and data platforms, they differentiated themselves in a commodity industry.

The lesson is consistent: companies that treat software as a strategic asset and build accordingly outperform those that treat it as a procurement decision.

## You Already Have the Foundation: Extend It

To be clear, this is not an argument for building your own ERP from scratch. That would be absurd. Most enterprises already have an ERP, whether it is Oracle, SAP, Microsoft Dynamics, or something else. They have run it for years, sometimes decades. They are not getting rid of it, and they should not. The ERP handles the core: financials, procurement, inventory, order management, HR. Those are solved problems, and the major vendors do them well enough.

The real argument is about what happens next. Once your ERP is in place, your business still has dozens of needs that sit outside what the ERP covers out of the box. Inspection workflows. Customer portals. Field service tracking. Specialized reporting. Approval systems tuned to your exact org structure. The question is how you fill those gaps.

The traditional answer has been to buy another COTS product. Need a field service solution? Buy one. Need a specialized quality management tool? Buy one. Need a customer self-service portal? Buy one. Each time, you bring in a team of consultants to implement it, configure it to approximate your business processes, integrate it with your ERP, and go live. And each time, you end up with something that is close to what you wanted but not exactly right.

**The economics of this approach are brutal.** Implementation consultants for enterprise COTS solutions bill $100 to $300 per hour, with enterprise-class firms charging $400 or more. A typical implementation runs 6 to 18 months. The software itself carries per-user licensing that grows with every new employee. Annual maintenance runs 18–22% of the original license cost, and ERP vendors earn 95% profit margins on those maintenance fees, with just 5% going to actual support delivery, according to Rimini Street's analysis. Then there are the inevitable customizations to make the COTS product fit your processes, which break during upgrades and require more consultant hours to fix.

Add it up over five years, and a single COTS add-on can easily cost $500,000 to $2 million or more, depending on complexity and user count. And you still do not have exactly what you wanted. You have a compromise.

**Now consider the alternative.** A senior full-time developer costs $150,000 to $200,000 per year fully loaded. Armed with AI coding tools, low-code platforms like Oracle APEX (which is free with your existing Oracle database license), and direct access to your ERP's data and APIs, that one developer can build custom extensions that fit your business exactly. Not approximately. Exactly. No consultant team. No per-user licensing that compounds annually. No maintenance fees flowing to a vendor's profit margins. No upgrade cycles that break your customizations.

Over that same five-year period, a full-time developer costs $750,000 to $1 million, and you own everything they build. The COTS route costs as much or more, and you own nothing. You are renting functionality, paying consultants to configure it, and hoping the vendor's roadmap continues to align with your needs.

This is the shift that AI makes possible. The gap between what your ERP does out of the box and what your business actually needs used to be expensive to close with custom code. It required large teams and long timelines. AI-assisted development has collapsed that cost. A small team, or even a single skilled developer with the right tools, can now build, test, and deploy ERP extensions at a pace that rivals COTS implementations, with the added benefit that the result is purpose-built for your organization.

**The companies seeing the best results are the ones that treat their ERP as a platform to build on, not a ceiling to live under.** They keep the ERP for what it does well, core transactions and record-keeping, and they build custom solutions for everything that differentiates their business. The ERP provides the data, the APIs, and the infrastructure. Custom development provides the fit.

## Real-World Examples: Extending the ERP

This is not hypothetical. Organizations across industries are already building custom extensions on top of their existing ERP platforms, and the results speak for themselves.

**A manufacturing company with 40+ plants** needed a way to digitize its entire galvanizing operation, from inspection workflows to customer-facing portals to environmental compliance tracking. No COTS product existed that fit their specific process. Instead of buying three or four separate tools and stitching them together, they built a [unified digital platform](/portfolio/digital-galvanizing-system) on Oracle APEX that integrated directly with their Oracle EBS instance. The result: near-total elimination of paper-based processes, real-time visibility across all facilities, iOS mobile apps for field workers, and a first-in-industry customer transparency portal. A COTS approach would have required multiple vendors, multiple integrations, and years of compromise. The custom approach delivered exactly what the business needed.

**A pharmaceutical research organization** was struggling with unreliable BI Publisher extracts to move data between Oracle Fusion Cloud and their analytics database. Extract failures were constant. Instead of buying a third-party ETL tool with its own licensing and support costs, they built an [automated integration using Oracle BICC](/portfolio/oracle-bicc-integration) with event-driven webhooks and OCI Object Storage. The result: 15-minute data refresh intervals, 90% reduction in extract failures, and comprehensive audit logging for compliance, all built on infrastructure they already owned.

**A luxury hospitality company** needed to integrate their Oracle Fusion financials with a third-party procurement system. The integration required real-time budget validation across a 7-segment Chart of Accounts, automated supplier synchronization, and GL code combination management. Buying a middleware product would have added another vendor, another license, and another support contract. Instead, they built targeted integrations using Oracle Integration Cloud, BI Publisher, and PL/SQL. One solution handles [automated budget delivery](/portfolio/birchstreet-budget-integration) with zero manual intervention. Another [synchronizes suppliers hourly](/portfolio/birchstreet-supplier-integration) with 100% accuracy. A third automatically [creates up to 255,000 GL code combinations](/portfolio/gl-code-combination-creator) to prevent validation errors. Three focused solutions, each doing exactly what the business needed, with no per-user licensing overhead.

**A financial services firm undergoing a bank acquisition** needed to automate GL journal imports during the transition, mapping a legacy chart of accounts to Oracle Fusion GL. This is exactly the kind of one-off but mission-critical need that no COTS vendor builds for. A custom [Oracle Integration Cloud solution](/portfolio/oic-gl-journal-import) handled daily automated processing with 100+ chart of accounts mappings, intelligent suspense handling, and real-time status notifications. Built in weeks, not months.

**An enterprise manufacturer** needed to track employee performance reviews, objectives, and multi-level approval workflows across global operations. HR software vendors would happily sell a solution for $30–50 per user per month. Instead, the company built a comprehensive [performance management platform on Oracle APEX](/portfolio/performance-management-platform), directly integrated with their existing Oracle EBS employee data. No additional licensing. No per-seat fees that scale with headcount. The system fits their exact review process, not the other way around.

The pattern across all of these examples is consistent: the ERP stays in place as the system of record. Custom extensions are built on top of it using tools like Oracle APEX, Oracle Integration Cloud, PL/SQL, and REST APIs, all of which are either included with existing licenses or available at minimal incremental cost. The custom solutions fit the business perfectly, cost less than COTS alternatives over time, and create no new vendor dependencies.

## A Balanced View: When COTS Still Makes Sense

Intellectual honesty demands acknowledging that COTS remains the right choice in certain scenarios:

**Commodity functions** like email, calendaring, and basic productivity tools are not worth building. Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace serve these needs well.

**Regulated, standardized processes** like accounting and payroll, where the rules are the same for everyone, benefit from COTS solutions that stay current with regulations.

**Speed-critical situations** where you need a solution next month, not next quarter, may still favor COTS as a starting point.

**Your ERP itself** is the clearest example. Nobody should build their own ERP. The argument is about what you build around it.

The argument is not that all COTS is bad. The argument is that AI has dramatically expanded the category of applications where building in-house, especially as extensions to your existing ERP platform, is now the superior choice: economically, strategically, and operationally.

## The Path Forward for Enterprise Leaders

For CTOs and CIOs evaluating their software strategy in 2026, the recommendation is clear:

**Audit your COTS portfolio.** Calculate the true total cost of ownership over 10 years, including licensing, support, customization, integration, and opportunity costs.

**Identify strategic applications.** Any software that touches your core differentiators, your customer experience, your proprietary processes, should be a candidate for in-house development.

**Invest in AI-augmented development capabilities.** Equip your teams with modern AI tools. The ROI is immediate and measurable.

**Adopt low-code platforms for rapid delivery.** Oracle APEX, Power Apps, and similar platforms let you build custom solutions quickly without sacrificing enterprise-grade security and scalability.

**Build incrementally.** You do not need to replace your entire COTS stack overnight. Start with one strategic application, prove the model, and expand.

The age of AI has not just made building easier. It has made buying riskier. As COTS vendors raise prices, deepen lock-in, and use AI to extract more revenue from their customers, the case for owning your own technology stack grows stronger every quarter.

The companies that recognize this shift early will have a significant, compounding advantage over those that remain locked into vendor roadmaps. The tools are ready. The economics have shifted. The question is whether your organization will seize the opportunity.
